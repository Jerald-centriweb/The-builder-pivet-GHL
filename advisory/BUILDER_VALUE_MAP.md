## The Builder's Day (What Life Actually Looks Like)

A custom builder doing 10–20 homes/year typically works like this:

**Morning:** Site visits, subcontractor management, project supervision
**Afternoon:** Client meetings, supplier quotes, compliance paperwork
**Evening:** Responding to enquiries, writing quotes, chasing unpaid invoices

The job is primarily operational. Sales is a side hustle they're not trained for and don't enjoy. Most builders became builders because they're good at building — not because they're good at selling.

### The Quoting Trap
When a homeowner enquiries, the builder's instinct is to be helpful. They schedule a site visit. They discuss ideas. They spend 3–8 hours preparing a detailed cost plan. They present it. The homeowner says "we'll think about it" and disappears.

This happens 3–5 times a month. That's 15–40 hours of unpaid work per month for many builders.

The builder knows this is wrong. They've complained about it at industry dinners for 20 years. But they keep doing it because:
1. They don't know how to ask for money without "seeming like they're just after money"
2. They're afraid of losing leads
3. They've never had a system that makes the fee conversation natural
4. Every competitor is still doing free quotes, so they feel they have to as well

### The Fee Framing Problem
The HIA preliminary agreement has existed for decades. Most builders know they should charge for preliminary work. Almost none of them do it consistently, because they frame it wrong:

**Wrong framing (why builders don't charge):**
"I need to charge you a fee before I can quote you." → sounds like the builder protecting themselves

**Right framing (what PreBuild Autopilot teaches):**
"Before we get into detailed numbers, we invest in a professional Budget Estimate for your project. You get a bankable cost range, a site assessment, and a clear brief — that's what the $550 covers. If we proceed to build, it comes off the project cost." → sounds like client-beneficial professional service

The system automates the right framing. The education sequence delivers it before the builder ever has to say it.

## What Builders Actually Measure Success By

| Metric | What they call it | Why it matters to them |
|--------|------------------|----------------------|
| Enquiry volume | "How many calls we're getting" | Survival anxiety — most builders fear lead drought |
| Quote-to-win rate | "How many we're converting" | The painful ratio — 80% of their quotes fail |
| Time per quote | Never tracked | They don't measure it, which is why the pain is invisible |
| Average project value | "The size of jobs we're getting" | Ambition metric — builders want bigger jobs |
| Referral rate | "Word of mouth" | Their most trusted channel — organic proof of quality |
| Payment reliability | "Chasing invoices" | Cash flow anxiety is constant |

**What they rarely measure (but should):**
- Cost of sales (time × rate × failed quotes)
- Lead source attribution (which channel produces paying clients)
- Lead quality by source (which channels produce tyre-kickers)

PreBuild Autopilot, once properly set up, generates all of these automatically.

## The Homeowner They're Trying to Attract

Target homeowner profile (what a hot lead looks like):

- Dual income couple, 30–50 years old
- Household income $150K–$350K
- Already owns land or has it under contract
- Has concept plans or has spoken to a designer
- Budget $600K–$1.5M (realistic for a custom build)
- Pre-approved or working with a broker
- Has spoken to 1–2 other builders (competitive but not over-shopped)
- Values quality over price — not looking for the cheapest quote

The system is explicitly designed to filter for this profile. The 13-question survey and scoring rubric identifies exactly this person and immediately books them with the builder.

### The Tyre-Kicker Profile (Who Gets Filtered Out)

- Budget under $300K for a new build in any metro area
- "Just exploring at this stage" (timeline 12+ months with no land)
- Has spoken to 5+ builders already (over-shopped, price-shopping)
- Not the decision-maker ("I need to ask my partner before we do anything")
- Refuses to pay a preliminary fee ("I only do free quotes")

The hard disqualifiers in the scoring engine catch the worst two categories immediately. The rest score low and go to nurture or not-now.

## Industry Language (Use This In Copy and Conversations)

### Terms builders use that mean something specific
- **Prelim** — preliminary agreement / preliminary services / preliminary fee (also the work done in Phase 1)
- **Tender** — detailed construction quote (Phase 2 output in some markets)
- **Volume builder** — large builder doing 50+ homes/year (NOT the target market)
- **Custom builder** — builds to a specific client brief each time (the target market)
- **KDR** — Knockdown Rebuild (buying an existing property to knock down and rebuild)
- **Spec build** — building a home to sell, not for a specific client (different sales dynamic)
- **DA** — Development Application (planning approval)
- **CC** — Construction Certificate (building approval)
- **HIA** — Housing Industry Association (trade association, provides contract templates)
- **MBA** — Master Builders Australia (the other major trade association)
- **Subbie** — subcontractor (plumber, electrician, concreter, etc.)

### Terms to avoid in builder conversations
- "CRM" — sounds like corporate software, not for them
- "Lead nurturing" — jargon they haven't heard
- "Marketing automation" — sounds expensive and complicated
- "Workflow" — internal term only
- "Pipeline" — OK to use but explain it as "your list of jobs in progress"

### Terms to use
- "System" — generic, non-threatening
- "Filter" — they understand filtering bad leads
- "Qualify" — they use this word themselves
- "Prelim" — if they use it, mirror it
- "Paid work" — always contrast quoting (unpaid) with prelim (paid)
- "Serious enquiry" — this is what they want, the phrase they use

## Questions to Ask Builders in a Discovery Call

Use these to qualify the builder AS a client, not just to sell them:

1. "How many enquiries would you say you get in a typical month?"
2. "Of those, roughly how many turn into paid work?"
3. "How long does it take you to put together a detailed quote?"
4. "Have you ever tried charging for preliminary work before?"
5. "What's your biggest frustration with the enquiry-to-client process right now?"
6. "Do you have a website that's currently generating leads, or is it mainly referrals?"
7. "Are you set up with Facebook Ads, or is that something you haven't tried yet?"
8. "What would it mean for your business if every lead that reached you had already committed $500 before talking to you?"

The last question is the one that gets silence, then "that would be amazing."
